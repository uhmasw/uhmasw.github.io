Monday, April 4, 2016

Arlan Brucal, University of Hawaii at Manoa, Economics Department

  • 12:30 PM - 1:20 PM

  • Saunders 515 - Miller Room

Not All States Are Alike: Measuring the Differential Effect of Oil

We study the effects of oil price shocks on economic activity at the US state level in the U.S. First, following Kilian (2015), we decomposed the real price of crude oil movements into three components: crude oil supply shocks; shocks to the global demand for all industrial commodities; and demand shocks that are specific to the crude oil market. We find that the implications of higher oil price to individual states’ income growth largely depends on the cause of that oil price increase. In particular, oil supply shocks and oil-specific demand shocks seem to influence state economies involved in producing oil. Industrial commodity demand shocks do not have significant effects on states’ growth, regardless of whether they are producing oil or not. Second, we measure the effect of economic spillovers across states and find that linear estimation ignoring potential spillovers tends to overestimate the impact of exogenous oil supply shocks. More importantly, major producers of oil are less affected by oil price drops through spillovers. To the extent that more recent crude oil price changes were driven by commodity demand shocks, our study helps to explain the reason why the macroeconomy responds very slowly to declining oil prices.

Camilo Mora, University of Hawaii at Manoa, Geography Department

  • 12:30 PM - 1:20 PM

Global human vulnerability to ongoing climate change

Climate change is one of the biggest stressors to the natural world, and one with multiple consequences on human welfare. In this presentation, I describe several recent research papers about the increasing human vulnerability to climate change, mediated by the impacts on nature. The results are not pretty but there is still hope to turn this around for the best.

Estimating Supply and Demand Elasticity for Fish: An Evaluation of Welfare Effects from Price Stabilization

Despite potentially large global gains, rights-based fishery management remains rare. One reason for this may that distributional welfare effects from adopting rights based management are largely unknown. This paper addresses this gap in the literature by considering the effects of price stabilization on producer and consumer surplus. Using daily market data from the Honolulu fish auction, I provide consistent estimates of price elasticity of supply and demand for bigeye tuna and broadbill swordfish. Results indicate that supply for both species is inelastic to price, while the price elasticity of demand for bigeye is -1.64, and for swordfish is -3.60. These parameter estimates suggest that price stabilization will benefit producers over consumers. I plan to empirically evaluate the distributional welfare effects using analytical approximations of producer and consumer surplus. These findings suggest that losses to consumer welfare may be a limitation to adopting rights-based management in fisheries.
